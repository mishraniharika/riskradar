A false positive is when the system flags a genuine transaction as fraud, while a false negative is when it misses actual fraud and lets it through. This tradeoff matters to a business because the two mistakes cost different things — too many false positives annoy real customers and hurt trust and revenue, while too many false negatives cost direct money through fraud losses, so a business has to decide which mistake it can tolerate more of rather than trying to eliminate both. Fraud systems rely on clues like transaction velocity (how many transactions a user makes in a short time) and amount deviation from the user's own history (a sudden large purchase compared to their usual spending), often combined with location/device mismatches, since no single clue proves fraud on its own. Accuracy is misleading here because fraud is rare — a model that predicts "not fraud" for everything can still score 99%+ accuracy while catching zero actual fraud, which is why precision, recall, and PR-AUC are used instead, since they show how well the model catches real fraud versus how many innocent users it wrongly blocks.
